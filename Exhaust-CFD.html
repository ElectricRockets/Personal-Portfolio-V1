Exhaust Manifold Acoustic CFD

Formula SAE

The OIT Formula SAE team faced challenges with their previous car, barely passing the competition's sound level requirements and experiencing engine idling issues due to high exhaust backpressure. To improve performance and ensure compliance, a new exhaust solution was needed that could significantly reduce noise without overly restricting engine power.

The core idea was to use computer simulations to explore various exhaust system designs before physical prototyping. By modeling different types of sound-reducing components like resonators and mufflers, we could predict their impact on both noise levels and engine backpressure.

Using Ansys Fluent Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software, we simulated several exhaust geometries attached to the base manifold. These included a straight pipe (baseline), single and dual resonators tuned to specific problem frequencies, an expansion chamber muffler, and a perforated reflector muffler. For each simulation, we measured the predicted Sound Pressure Level (SPL) at the standard FSAE test point and the average cylinder backpressure.

The simulations revealed a clear trade-off: designs offering the most sound reduction often created the most backpressure. For instance, the expansion chamber achieved a large 22.6 dB noise drop but added a significant 14.98 kPa of backpressure. Conversely, the single resonator was very efficient, reducing noise by 6.3 dB while only increasing backpressure by 1.34 kPa, primarily targeting the loudest frequency.

Based on the results, a hybrid design was recommended. Combining a resonator to efficiently tackle the main problematic frequency with an expansion chamber to handle broader noise reduction offers the best balance. This approach aims to meet the required sound level reduction while mitigating the significant backpressure penalty associated with using only a high-restriction muffler.
